Page 03

Purpose:
The limitation — human-crafted memory designs are fixed. A box hand-drawn for one kind of task breaks when the task changes; it can't adapt to diverse, non-stationary problems.

Audience note:
Builds on page 2. "Design" = the rules for what the memory stores, how it's retrieved, and how it's updated.

Panel Count:
6

Image Prompt:

Create a six-panel educational manga page.

Style:
Soft-color educational manga.
Light watercolor / gentle anime palette — warm cream paper, soft ink linework, muted pastel washes.
One restrained accent: recall-green (#00e0a4), used only for memory, recall, and "it worked" moments.
Clean confident linework, generous negative space, friendly but precise.
Calm, contemplative, explanatory tone — diagrams integrated naturally into the scene.
Not gritty. Not neon. Not chibi. Not glossy 3D. Not photorealistic.

Characters in this page:
- ENGY: a small, rounded librarian-robot with a soft matte-cream chassis, one big friendly camera-lens eye that glows a faint recall-green, short stubby arms, and an open NOTEBOOK embedded in its chest — its memory.
- SENSEI: a calm middle-aged human archivist with round glasses, a soft cardigan over a high-collar shirt, neat greying hair, holding a slim wooden pointer; here shown hand-drawing a memory design on a chalkboard.

Setting:
A chalkboard wall. Sensei draws a memory "blueprint" by hand. Several different task-worlds are sketched as small icons (a kitchen, a word-puzzle, a tile-game, a dungeon).

Panel 1 (medium):
Sensei carefully hand-draws a memory blueprint on the board — neat boxes and arrows — clearly bespoke for one task (a kitchen-chores icon). Pride in the craft. Dialogue space.

Panel 2 (close):
Engy's chest-notebook reshapes to match the hand-drawn blueprint; it fits the kitchen task perfectly — green glow, success.

Panel 3 (medium, new world):
A very different task appears (a tile-puzzle world). The same rigid blueprint is forced onto it — the boxes don't fit; arrows point nowhere. Engy looks confused.

Panel 4 (close, failure):
The notebook strains against the mismatched design; pages jam, the green flickers and dims. The fixed design is the bottleneck now.

Panel 5 (two-shot):
Sensei steps back from the board, chalk dust on hands, acknowledging the limit. Many different worlds, one fixed shape — it can't fit them all. Dialogue space.

Panel 6 (wide, the turn):
A row of diverse task-worlds, each wanting a *different* memory shape. Sensei lowers the pointer thoughtfully — what if the design didn't have to be drawn by hand at all?

Camera directions:
Steady, instructional; contrast the perfect fit (panel 2, green) with the jammed mismatch (panels 3–4, dimmed); end on the wide field of diverse worlds to motivate page 4.

Lettering:
Leave room for English dialogue and captions.
- Panel 1 — Sensei: "For years, *we* drew the memory design by hand — what to store, how to fetch it, when to update."
- Panel 2 — (caption) "Hand-crafted for one task, it fits beautifully."
- Panel 3 — Engy: "This world is... different. Nothing lines up."
- Panel 4 — (caption) "A *fixed* design can't adapt to new, changing tasks."
- Panel 5 — Sensei: "Diverse, shifting tasks each want a different memory. One hand-drawn shape can't serve them all."
- Panel 6 — Sensei: "So... what if the design didn't have to be drawn by hand?"

Output:
A single finished manga page.
